In the Pursuit of Language

Langauge acquisition often refers to the process of learning (or acquiring) a language through natural processes. This is often the process with which young children gain the ability to speak their native tongue. No grammar rules, no formal classrooms, and no graded assignments. It is one of the many miracles of our human existence, and it has long been something that has absolutely captured my attention.

As a result of this fascination, the question that has been on my mind is how I, as an adult, can replicate the process of native language acquisition in my pursuit of becoming a polyglot.

Please read below to see some of my own thoughts and devised strategies on how I can acquire language naturally. I have also included my list of languages that currently hold my interest and a little bit about their origin countries.



Strategies for Language Acquisition

  1. Media
    • Books - Books often offer a natural view of language and its written form. Diving into a book in a foreign language gives you a sense of that langauges natural grammatical structure and syntax without typical grammar lessons.
    • Videos and Movies - Watching media of native speakers of your target language is probably one of the most important things you could incorporate if you do not have a real life native speaker at your disposal. This approach will give you a sense of how the tones of your target lagnuage shift and change during speech and it gives you the luxury of pausing and rewinding in the event of misunderstandings.
    • Music - This beautiful tool not only makes natural language acquisition fun, but it also allows you to dive into the colloquial sayings, idioms, metaphors, and similes that make a language colorful and unique.

  2. Friends
    • Real native speakers are simply the most efficient way to learn a langauge. If one is not immersed in their target language, they will find it difficult to keep that knowledge at the forefront of their lives. Further, if a learner has no friends with whom to practice the language in addition to not being immersed in that langauge, it increases the difficulty to a much higher degree. Although it sounds obvious, the importance of friends (native speakers or fellow learners) cannot be overstated.

  3. Thoughts
    • This point may sound silly or obscure, still I have learned from personal experience that it is one of the most useful and easy to implement. When one finds that they lack access to media or fellow speakers of their target language, what else can be done to practice and run through vocabulary? The only simple answer is to practice thinking in that language. Shifting one's thoughts to view the world through the lense of a different tongue can be awkward at first, but it is a necessary step that each learner must take if they hope to acheive proficiency in that new tongue. A learner should start simply by naming things that they see every day in their new language, and let it progress into sentences fromt their as they build their vocabularly and knowledge of the langauge's structure.
